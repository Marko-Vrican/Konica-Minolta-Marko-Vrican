In plain terms

For every 10 customers on our books, 2 will walk away this year. Replacing them costs 5× more than keeping them — so every prevented exit is a direct hit to the bottom line. And the losses concentrate in segments we can identify in advance.

What happens if we do nothing

The bleed is recurring. If churn holds at 20% year over year, the bank must acquire two new customers for every ten already on the books — just to stay flat. Add the 5× cost of acquisition vs. retention, and the compounded loss over three years reaches an estimated ~18M €. Every month without an intervention makes the problem larger, not smaller.

What management needs to know

1

Peak risk is specific

Women aged 50–59 churn at 63% — the highest single-cell in the entire matrix. This segment needs a dedicated retention play.

2

Over-selling backfires

Once a customer holds 3+ products, churn is nearly certain. The current cross-sell strategy is a headwind, not a lever.

3

Germany underperforms

Churn in Germany is twice as high as in France or Spain. Requires a market-specific commercial review.

4

Inactivity is a warning

Inactive members churn at nearly double the rate. Re-engagement is the cheapest form of retention we have.
