Step 3 — The Solution

An AI model that predicts churn before it happens.

We tested five machine-learning approaches on the customer data. The best model — Gradient BoostingAn algorithm that builds hundreds of small decision trees in sequence. Each new tree corrects the mistakes of the previous ones. Result: strong predictions and clear feature importance. — identifies at-risk customers with 87% accuracy. Retention teams can now act on individual predictions instead of reacting after the fact.

In plain terms — two questions the model answers

78%
PrecisionOf all customers the model flags as at-risk, the percentage who truly are. High precision = focused, non-wasteful outreach.
"When the model rings the alarm, is it right?"
Out of every 100 customers the model flags, 78 will genuinely leave. Retention calls land on real risk — not on random customers.
50%
RecallOf all customers who actually leave, the percentage the model flags in advance. High recall = few missed exits, but more false alarms.
"Does the model catch everyone who leaves?"
Out of every 100 customers who actually exit, the model catches 50 in advance. The other half slip through — a deliberate trade-off.
Why the trade-off: a stricter model catches fewer exits but never wastes a call. A more aggressive one catches everyone but wastes many. Ours is tuned toward precision — every flagged customer is worth the outreach. The threshold is not fixed: the business can dial it toward higher recall (catch more, accept more false alarms) whenever strategy demands.

Honest limitations

The model predicts the "who", not the "why". It tells retention teams which customers are about to leave — but the conversation itself still decides whether they stay. Human skill converts a flag into a save.

Patterns drift over time. The model was trained on the current customer base. New products, market shifts or a new competitor can change behaviour, so it should be re-trained at least quarterly to stay accurate.

No shortcuts under the hood. One field (Complain) perfectly matched the outcome — we removed it before training. The 87% accuracy is earned on genuine signals, not on a data leak.

What to do next

Actions the model directly supports, prioritised by expected business impact.

1

Launch a 40–59 retention programme

Mid-career customers churn at 30–63%. Design targeted loyalty offers, personal account managers or life-event benefits (mortgage, education, retirement planning) for this segment. Prioritise women 50–59 first — they are the single riskiest cell.

Highest ROI
2

Cap cross-selling at two products

Customers with 3+ products almost always leave. Review whether these bundles carry hidden fees, complexity or friction. Two products is the retention sweet spot — the model confirms this quantitatively.

Structural fix
3

Automate a weekly at-risk list

Run the model every Monday and deliver a ranked list of the top 200 customers most likely to leave. Focused outreach beats mass campaigns — 78% of flagged customers are genuinely at risk, so calls convert.

Operational win
4

Run a Germany deep-dive

Germany's churn rate (32%) is double the other markets — a market signal, not a product one. Commission a commercial review to test whether pricing, product-market fit or a specific competitor is behind the gap.

Market-specific
5

Re-engage inactive members

Inactive customers churn at 27% versus 14% for active ones. A structured re-engagement campaign — personal calls, tailored offers, a light-touch onboarding refresh — is the cheapest form of retention available.

Low cost
